Bayesian Regret and VSE

Bayesian Regret is a method that can be used to evaluate voting systems. Utilities are assigned to every candidate for every voter in the simulation, and these utilities are used to a) construct voter profiles, and b) determine the total utility for every candidate if they win the election. These utilities can be assigned using spatial methods, wherein voters and candidates can be assigned position vectors where each element is a numerical representation of a person's stance on some issue, and utility is the Euclidean distance between voters and candidates. A 'magic winner' is determined, as some candidate whose position is such that the total utility is a maximum. Elections are run for various voting methods, and the utility is given by the winner; this number is then compared to the magic utility, and the difference between the two is the Bayesian Regret. Lots of elections (simulations) are run to find the average Bayesian Regret per voting system. To make this more informative, different voter behaviours can be involved.

For example, complete voter honesty where voter profiles are proportional to utility profiles, fully-strategic voting, one-sided strategic, two-sided strategic, or a mix. VSE (voter satisfaction efficiency) is slightly different to Bayesian regret: \[VSE(method) = 1 - {BR(method) \over BR(random winner)}\]

Quinn's simulation is a bit more recent and more sophisticated than Smith's; beyond more voting methods, and more voter behaviours, he also includes random noise in the conversion from utility profile to voter profile (because voters don't really know their exact positions, or candidates exact positions, and may have other biases). His utility generation method is also a bit more realistic.

In doing non-trivial, I made a (very primitive) model to try and do this better, and found some areas where I think the model could be refined to be more informative:

  • How many issues do voters actually take into account when making decisions?
  • How should the position vector elements be generated? i.e. randomly, normally (with what parameters?) Should these elements be independent?
  • What voter behaviours are the most common / what proportions do they occur in?
  • Should random noise be added by drawing randomly from some given range, or using some distribution?
